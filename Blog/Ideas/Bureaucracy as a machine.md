Which raises the question that has teeth: a system that genuinely produced gains would tend to grow legible metrics for them, because someone would want the credit. Persistent unmeasurability of a system's value is itself evidence — weak but real — pointing one of two ways. Either the gains aren't there and the opacity is what lets everyone avoid finding out, or the unmeasurability is load-bearing for someone. The cynical reading, and I'll flag it as the cynical reading because it's seductive and it's your own Gervais frame: the person who championed the transformation banks the credit at launch and is gone before the flat productivity number arrives, and the opacity is precisely what protects them from being held to the gains they promised. I don't assert that's what's happening. I assert that "we can't see the gains" should never be treated as a neutral epistemic limit. It's a place to dig, and the dig is: who benefits from it staying unmeasured
