 I've been worried about what AI is doing to my own learning. The pattern that worries me most shows up in the research on students: better grades in the short term, less understanding underneath, and a harder time working on their own once the tool is taken away.

The convenience is appealing, and I don't think that's a weakness in people. Being able to make something without first grinding through the tedious work that used to stand in the way is a real shift. The tedium was often the barrier, and now a lot of it is optional. There are plenty of honest reasons someone can't spend years building a foundation but still wants to make something that helps them in their life.

What you lose, when you skip the hours of learning a building skill like programming, is the friction. Wrestling with the tools and the language is also how you come to understand them. But here's the honest problem with that worry. We've been climbing the abstraction ladder for decades. The first programming languages were low-level, and the programmer had to track things that modern languages now handle for you. Nobody mourns hand-managing memory anymore. So if every rung up the ladder has been fine, why would this one be different? Maybe AI is just the next rung, and the loss I'm describing is only nostalgia.

But there's a second question hiding behind the first. Are you building the thing, or are you building yourself? Those aren't opposites, and they aren't the same thing either. You can do both at once, or one without the other. What's changed is that they used to come bundled. To make the thing, you had to build yourself a little along the way, because the friction didn't leave you much choice. AI pulls them apart. Now you can have the thing with almost none of the self attached. That separation isn't a loss by itself. It only costs you when the self-building was the point and you let the tool skip it without noticing. Our imagination and inventiveness are built from problems we've actually wrestled with, and the work you skip now is the understanding you won't have in ten years.

The tool isn't going anywhere, so the question isn't whether to use it. It's whether I want to be changed by the work, or just want it done. The friction was an encounter. You turn toward it, you wrestle with it, and that is part of what shapes you. Hand it off and the work still gets done, but it doesn't form me, because I was never there for it. So when the work is something I want to be shaped by, I keep the friction. When it isn't, I let the tool carry it.

This blog is the test case, and it isn't clean. You've probably noticed it carries AI fingerprints. The harder one to admit is the writing. I work through these posts with Claude, back and forth, and it helps. It challenges me, it finds the holes, it gives my thoughts a structure other people can follow. The ideas are mine. Every post comes from something I've been chewing on for a while, and when it frames something in a way I don't quite agree with, I correct it. What I get from the back and forth is friction and form, a cleaner version of a thought I already had. The one thing I watch is that a well-shaped suggestion is a little seductive, easy to accept because it reads well even when it's half a step off from what I meant, and refusing that version when it isn't mine is part of the work.
 
The images are the easy case by comparison. They're made with Gemini, and I have no quarrel with that, because I never set out to become a digital illustrator. I want a picture that tells a specific story alongside a post, and working with AI to make one is faster and lands better than spending months on a craft that was never the mission. Nothing in me was supposed to be built by drawing it, so nothing is lost by generating it.

The worry I started with was about the tool. However, I don't think that's where the risk actually lives. I think it lives in my choices. What I let grab my attention and time, and what I let form me. Whether I keep the friction that was supposed to shape me, or quietly let it go.

There has been a lot of stories, opinions and research on the drawbacks of using AI. For example some stories suggest that the increased usage of LLMs is making it so that students get better grades short term but long term they lose understanding and don't learn as well on their own.

This is something that concerns me with my own learning too. I think the convenience of AI tools is something that is very appealing to people in general, and being able to create without some of the tedious work that used to be a hinderance on the way to creation of the end product is a game changer. There can be many reasons on why it is not feasible for many people to put in the hours to grind the foundations, while still wanting to produce something that helps them in their life.

What you do lose without putting in the hours of learning a "building" skill like programming is the engagement with the tools and the language on a certain level. Although we have moved up the abstraction level over time, compared to the very first generation of programming languages, who were lower-level. Most of the considerations programmers had to take into account back then many more modern programming languages solve for you. This means that you can still build without having to understand to intricacies of lower level implementations.

I think that the implication for builders and learners is that we have to be mindful of what we are building. With speed often comes negligence for some details. That can be okay for a personal tool, maybe not so much for critical systems. Also the question becomes are you building yourself? Our understanding, imagination and inventiveness is much based on prior experiences we have had and the problems, experiences and patterns in life we have come across. Shallow work and less engagement means that a lot less deep understanding will take root in you. This will affect any future work you will do tomorrow or the next ten years. 

Maybe the challenge for many learners or builders is to find a sweet spot between speed and deep engagement. I don't think the LLMs will go away or be obsolete any time soon. The question is given this new landscape how do we engage with it? 

What I am trying to do is to balance my own usage in various areas in my life. Take this blog as an example, you may have noticed this blog is not absent of AI fingerprints. The images for the blog posts is generated with Gemini, I found that it's way easier and impactful for me to tell a specific story with a picture that can I work with AI to produce rather than learning the skill of making those myself, as the mission for this blog was not to learn to make digital illustrations.

Another thing you as a reader might have noticed is that I have worked with AI to help me finish my blog posts and also to keep a structure that others can digest. Maybe this might fact does make the blog feel lower value to some, but my goals with this blog is to share ideas, perspectives and reflections that I have and to grow in the process of wrestling with ideas to figure out what I actually believe. Every post comes from me having had an engagement with the topic for a while and putting my own twist to it, but I have learnt that sometimes I have to slow down and structure my thoughts and working with Claude through that process is helping me deliver something to the first and foremost myself, but also to anyone else that might be interested in what I have to say.

Maybe I want to end on that note with this post that the important thing to consider is to be mindful of the tradeoffs you are making for speed, I will never become a digital illustrator by generating images with Gemini, but maybe I will produce a product that will impact me and others and that is all good with me.
