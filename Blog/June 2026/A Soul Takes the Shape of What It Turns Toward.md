We'd like to think of ourselves as independent thinkers or original creatives. "I would be the same even if I was born in Morocco in the 1800s", feels like it's true. I think the other hypothetical Moroccan you would feel the same.

The reason you'd both feel that certainty is the same: you only ever experience the sliver of reality you attend to, and that sliver is all your brain gets to call real. That sliver differs from person to person. What we attend to leaves a trace; attend to the same things often enough and the trace hardens into a lens, the thing through which the next moment gets seen. So over time, what you notice becomes who does the noticing. Attention isn't just how you see the world; it's how the world settles into you.

So the current you, the one deciding what's worth your attention, is downstream of everything the past you attended to. The evaluator is itself a product of what it's evaluating. There is no version of you standing outside this loop. In essence, you are both the sculptor and the stone.

With this perspective it's easy to feel trapped, stuck in a loop you didn't choose, especially if circumstances haven't been kind. And from inside the moment, that's true: you can't simply decide to pay attention to better things, because the you doing the deciding is the one the old inputs built. But you're not stuck inside the moment. You can change what surrounds you, and what surrounds you slowly builds who you become. That's the lever. Not willing yourself to focus, but choosing the rooms you stand in, and standing in better ones over time.

Nowhere is this more loaded than in the attention economy we now live inside. Feeds, ads, the endless click-chase, these aren't neutral surroundings; they're rooms engineered to hold you, built by people whose interests aren't yours. A lot of smart people respond with digital minimalism: cut it back, delete the apps, go quiet. The subtraction helps: it gives us opportunities to more easily shift focus, but we still have to point it somewhere else, digital or not.

When I set out to become better at programming for the second time, I didn't just study it; I tried to engineer the surroundings so the learning would happen to me. I had structured courses on boot.dev, and I subscribed to programming creators on YouTube, not just the tutorials but the entertainment, so that even when I wasn't trying, the feed was still pointed at code. I wasn't escaping the recommendation engine; I was trying to aim it. I also built my own small tools, and each one made me into someone who could see the next. I was creating positive associations, building a reinforcing loop around an identity I wanted to live inside before I could prove I deserved it, which is, I think, the only way anyone grows into one. And it worked: I started building my own things; small tools, websites, things I now use at work.

Picture that other Jonathan, born in Morocco in the 1800s. He wakes before dawn, works the trade his father worked, prays when everyone around him prays, and is every bit as certain as I am that he sees the world as it plainly is. His attention was fed by what surrounded him, and it built a man who'd find my certainties as strange as I find his. That's the point: I wouldn't be the same person born into his world. There's no essential me that survives the swap — context shapes the person, every time. But context isn't only something that happens to you. It's also something you can reach back and adjust. You can't choose where you begin. But you are not only where you began, and from here, some of what builds you next is yours to choose.

One of the reasons this is not true is that our brains are neuroplastic, meaning they can re-wire themselves based on new experiences. Given this fact I have started to think about the fact that our attention is what feeds our brains re-wiring. The nature of attention means that we can only pay attention to that is in our vicinity or that we are aware of.

There is a feedback loop in our brains, new inputs get interpreted and then molds our reaction to our next experience, and so on. Some relations begins forming stronger, strengthening and solidifying our internal world. Given the new attention economy of our time where everyone fights for our attention (ads, clicks in the digital landscape), that gives us much to think about with regards to our attention diet.

I do think it takes us to see outside ourselves and become aware of the biological mechanism of neuroplasticity and attention, believing and wanting the best for our future selves.
