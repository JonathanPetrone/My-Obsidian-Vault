The first month, I had momentum. A long backlog of topics I wanted to cover, everything I had taken in from a year or two of reading and chasing whatever caught my attention. The posts almost wrote themselves, because the material was already there, waiting.
  
This week was harder. Nothing came. And that emptiness got me thinking about where the urge to write actually comes from, and how to find it again.
  
It didn't take me long to land on a post about the etymology of the word inspire. It comes from the Latin inspirare, literally "to breathe into." In its early usage the meaning settled into something more bodily: to draw air into the lungs.
  
That's it, exactly.

Work has taken my thoughts and my energy, and the blog has asked me to produce rather than take in. Everything in my days right now is an exhale. I have been breathing out for weeks, and no wonder the lungs are empty.
  
When you are in a stressful stretch, all of your energy goes to keeping your head above water. Forming new ideas, noticing connections, none of that happens on its own when you are running at that level.

So the obvious move is to schedule the intake. Block time to read, be deliberate about it. Which I tried, but I didn't feel as good about it as I used to. The joy was gone.

And the absence told me more than the reading did, because the joy was never incidental. It was how I knew the reading was reaching me. The moment I put it on the calendar it became another task, another thing to get through. I had turned the in-breath into one more exhale and called it rest.

Which makes me wonder if I have had this backwards this whole week. I have been treating the silent week as a failure, a gap in the consistency I was proud of. But you cannot only breathe out. The pause is not the opposite of the writing. It is the part that makes the next writing possible. The breath comes in on its own time, not mine.
