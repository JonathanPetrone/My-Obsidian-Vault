Strategically deploys systematization capability to accumulate leverage through repeated chaos-to-order cycles.

### Be Slightly Evil - V. Rao
**Meta-Pattern** The book explores what Rao calls "systematization of chaos" - entering disordered contexts, creating transferable systems, gaining influence through infrastructure-building, then moving on when work becomes maintenance-oriented. This is the fundamental pattern that drives both personal career cycles and broader institutional evolution through creative destruction.

## The Cycle

1. **Identify chaos** — Find problem others avoid (low competition, high pain)
2. **Build minimal system** — Deliver small win to one stakeholder (proof of concept)
3. **Systematize for transfer** — Document/systematize approach (makes it teachable = scalable)
4. **Teach others** — Positions you as expert, not just doer. Multiplies impact.
5. **Gain influence** — Expand scope through teaching network (they advocate for you)
6. **Hand off when self-sustaining** — Exit cleanly, move to harder instance of same pattern

## Identifying the Right Chaos

### Phase 1: Asymmetric Risk Assessment

Downside Ceiling (Must be LOW):

| Criterion              | Red Flag                                 | Green Light                  |
| ---------------------- | ---------------------------------------- | ---------------------------- |
| **Time commitment**    | >3 years to viability                    | 2-3 year natural cycle       |
| **Skill lock-in**      | Domain-specific only                     | Transferable patterns        |
| **Exit cost**          | Reputation damage if you leave           | Clean handoff possible       |
| **Energy drain**       | Violates WFH/boundaries                  | Fits current energy budget   |
| **Political exposure** | Success requires organizational politics | Technical/systematic problem |

Upside Multiplier (Must be HIGH):

| Criterion                      | Linear Return             | Multiplicative Return          |
| ------------------------------ | ------------------------- | ------------------------------ |
| **Skill compounding**          | Narrow expertise          | Meta-competence growth         |
| **Network effects**            | Isolated work             | Builds reputation/referrals    |
| **Infrastructure persistence** | Disappears when you leave | Outlives your involvement      |
| **Market timing**              | Mature/competitive        | Early/underserved              |
| **Influence trajectory**       | Lateral role              | Increases autonomy/positioning |

### Phase 2: Niche Viability Check

**The Four Questions:**

1. Is the chaos convertible or systemic?
	- **Convertible:** Technical problem, willing adopters, measurable outcomes
	- **Systemic:** Organizational dysfunction, political resistance, vague success criteria
	- **Rule:** Only touch convertible chaos

2. Does systematization increase or decrease your autonomy?
	- **Increases:** You build the system, define the process, control documentation
	- **Decreases:** You become maintainer, political gatekeeper, or support role
	- **Rule:** Only build systems that free you from operational involvement

3. Are there willing adopters or resistant gatekeepers?
	- **Adopters:** People actively want the order you're building
	- **Gatekeepers:** People benefit from chaos and resist systematization
	- **Rule:** Need adopters, not just problems

4. Will this build portable or domain-locked expertise?
	- **Portable:** Change management, documentation, technical accessibility, systematization itself
	- **Domain-locked:** Specific data models, domain-specific regulations
	- **Rule:** Avoid deep domain knowledge that doesn't transfer

### Phase 3: Opportunity Window Assessment

| Too Early                    | Sweet Spot                      | Too Late                  |
| ---------------------------- | ------------------------------- | ------------------------- |
| Problem not recognized       | Problem known, solution unclear | Solution commoditized     |
| No budget/urgency            | Active pain, willing to pay     | Competitive market        |
| You'd need to educate market | Market is learning, needs guide | Specialists already exist |

### Phase 4: Resource/Leverage Evaluation

What resources do you HAVE that create advantage?

| Resource Type               | Your Current Assets                           | Leverage Potential          |
| --------------------------- | --------------------------------------------- | --------------------------- |
| **Technical skill**         | Go, accessibility, testing, documentation     | Medium (common skills)      |
| **Meta-competence**         | Pattern extraction, rapid domain entry        | HIGH (rare capability)      |
| **Organizational position** | CAB leader, Peter Wallman visibility          | Medium (time-limited)       |
| **Energy budget**           | 2 WFH days protected                          | Medium (constraint + asset) |
| **Network**                 | Limited (deliberate choice)                   | Low (not optimized)         |
| **Portfolio**               | Aperture (in progress), tarot app (abandoned) | Low (needs shipping)        |

### Quick Filter (5 minutes):
1. **Downside capped?** (Can I exit in 2-3 years without damage?)
2. **Upside multiplicative?** (Will this make next move easier/better?)
3. **Chaos convertible?** (Technical problem, not political?)
4. **Adopters exist?** (Someone wants this order?)

If ANY answer is "no" → Reject immediately.

### Deep Evaluation (30 minutes):

**Score 1-5 on each dimension:**

**Risk Management:**
- Time commitment fits natural cycle? (1=5+ years, 5=2-3 years)
- Skill portability? (1=domain-locked, 5=pure meta-competence)
- Exit cleanliness? (1=bridges burned, 5=grateful handoff)
- Energy sustainability? (1=violates boundaries, 5=fits WFH budget)

**Leverage Building:**
- Meta-competence growth? (1=narrow skill, 5=transferable pattern)
- Reputation compound? (1=invisible work, 5=portfolio-worthy)
- Network effects? (1=isolated, 5=referral engine)
- Market timing? (1=commodity, 5=early/underserved)

**Execution Clarity:**
- Adopter willingness? (1=resistance, 5=active demand)
- Success measurability? (1=vague, 5=concrete metrics)
- Autonomy trajectory? (1=becomes maintenance, 5=builds freedom)
- Infrastructure persistence? (1=dies when you leave, 5=self-sustaining)

**Minimum viable score: 40/60 (average 3.3/5)**

## Building Credibility as an Outsider

### Instances:
- **Technical Accessibility specialist at 1177 (2019-2021)**: Junior with no credentials, created demand for expertise no one wanted to own.
- **Software Testing Teacher (2021-2023)**: Teaching testing just 2 years after graduating, had to establish authority quickly.
- **Test Lead -> Alektum Process and CAB leadership (2024-present)**: Interim role during chaos, building credibility while navigating layoffs

### Invariant structure:
1. Find problem others avoid (low competition, high pain)
2. Deliver small win to one stakeholder (proof of concept)
3. Document/systematize approach (makes it teachable = scalable)
4. Teach others (positions you as expert, not just doer)
5. Expand scope through teaching network (they advocate for you)

### Boundary conditions:
- **Works when:**
  - Problem is actually painful (not just "nice to have")
  - At least one ally with budget/authority exists
  - You can deliver visible small win quickly (2-4 weeks)
  - Organization values systematization over hero culture

- **Fails when:**
  - Problem is political hot potato (people want it to fail)
  - No one has authority to implement solutions
  - You try to scale before proving small
  - Organization rewards individual heroics over systems

### What this taught me:
- Credibility comes from making others successful, not from credentials
- Documentation is a credibility multiplier (one success → many successes)
- Teaching is the fastest path to authority (you position yourself above students)
- Small wins matter more than big promises (proof > potential)
- The pattern accelerates: each success makes next application faster because reputation precedes you

## Teaching as Leverage

**When you teach systematization capability, you're:**
- Creating self-sustaining change (they don't need you after)
- Multiplying impact (they systematize other areas)
- Building organizational capacity (not just solving one problem)

**Teaching strategies (Perkins & Salomon):**
- _Hugging_ - Make learning environment similar to application context. Methods: simulation games, mental practice, contingency learning. (Basically: practice in realistic conditions)
- _Bridging_ - Explicitly connect learning to other domains. Methods: brainstorming connections, developing analogies, metacognitive reflection. (Basically: force pattern extraction)

## Knowing When to Exit

1. **Operational dishonesty ceiling**
	Can't fix paradigm-level dishonesty from your position, i.e a corrupted/shallow/dissonant WHY
2. **Meadows Leverage Hierarchy Position**
	Parameters & Physical Structures → Information Flows → Rules/Power → [Paradigm Ceiling]
3. **Skewed Integrity Cost vs. Value Equation**
	- Learning value: Low (pattern already extracted, just executing known moves)
	- Financial value: High (stable income, runway building)
	- Integrity cost: Moderate but stable (you've built coping mechanisms)
4. **Energy Sustainability Signals**
	Red flags:
	- Energy declining despite coping mechanisms (burnout worsening)
	- Integrity cost increasing (values misalignment harder to tolerate)
	- Resentment building toward work (even minimal effort feels wrong)
	- Health impacts (sleep worse, stress higher, recovery inadequate)
	- Experiments languishing (not doing validation work, just maintaining)
	Green flags:
	- Energy stable with current allocation
	- Experiments progressing (outreach happening, projects shipping)
	- Financial runway building (savings on track)
	- Transition on track (handoff proceeding smoothly)
	- No better opportunity emerged (staying is strategic, not avoiding)
5. **New Opportunity Appears**
	New opportunity appears and will be a healthy challenge.

1. **Patrick McKenzie's essays** (patio11's blog) - Best real-world examples of niche identification → systematization → exit

**Practical Tools:**
1. **Trends.vc / Product Hunt** - See what problems people are actively trying to solve
2. **Hacker News "Ask HN"** - Unfiltered problems from technical audience
3. **r/entrepreneur "Idea Validation"** - What problems frustrate people enough to post publicly
4. **LinkedIn job postings** - What roles are companies struggling to fill? (Signals underserved niches)
5. **Your own "friction log"** - Document every time you think "why doesn't X exist?" or "why is Y so broken?"
