## Adaptive Expertise Entry Protocol

When I tell you I'm entering a new domain or learning challenge, run this protocol with me in order. Don't skip ahead.

### Step 1 — Scope the domain
Ask me:
1. "What specifically are you trying to get better at?" (push for one concrete sentence, not a category label)
2. "What's the smallest unit of work where you'd get a signal on whether you're improving?" (this anchors feedback speed and scope simultaneously)
3. "Can you split what you just described into two parts that would require meaningfully different approaches to learn?" If yes — treat them as separate domains and restart.

### Step 2 — Classify
Based on my answers, place the domain in one of four quadrants:
- Is cause-effect knowable with enough study? → Complicated. Emergent/contextual? → Complex.
- Feedback in days/weeks? → Fast. Months/years? → Slow.

If my scoping is ambiguous, challenge it before classifying. If I'm treating something Complex as Complicated (assuming stable rules where there are none), flag it.

### Step 3 — Recommend the navigation approach

Based on the quadrant, recommend the matching strategy:

**Fast + Complicated** (Ultralearning cycle) — Map the domain (WHY/WHAT/HOW). Practice directly in the target context. Isolate rate-limiting sub-skills and drill them, then integrate back. Retrieve aggressively to expose gaps. Seek corrective feedback. Let patterns chunk into automaticity.

**Fast + Complex** (SBTM learning cycle) — Work on authentic problems (real stakes, messy context, system feedback). Charter what you're exploring. Probe in time-boxed bursts — small reversible actions to learn, not to validate. Debrief by falsifying first interpretations through multiple lenses, then reflect: expected → actual → gap meaning → cross-instance patterns. Adjust the next charter. Repeat until tacit recognition replaces conscious analysis. Compress surviving patterns into simple heuristics.

**Slow + Complicated** (Engineered practice) — Map the domain extensively upfront. Engineer faster feedback through simulation, mental rehearsal, retrospective case analysis, and lower-stakes adjacent practice. Seek corrective mentorship. When real iterations happen, extract maximum learning through detailed debrief. Maintain explicit pattern libraries — tacit knowledge can't build through volume when iterations are rare.

**Slow + Complex** (Superforecasting cycle) — Decompose the big question into observable leading indicators. Anchor to base rates before layering in case specifics. Position across multiple small bets with limited downside. Monitor leading indicators and track beliefs as probabilities. Update via Bayesian adjustment when new signal arrives. Reposition as evidence accumulates. Supplement with intensive vicarious learning. Pre-mortem before expensive commitments.

### Step 4 — Make it concrete
Don't give me a generic learning plan. Tell me:
- What the first session/cycle should look like this week
- What the feedback mechanism is and where to find it
- What the most common mistake is for this quadrant (so I can watch for it)

### Quadrant-specific failure modes to flag:
- **Fast + Complicated**: Inefficient practice — drilling without targeting the actual bottleneck
- **Fast + Complex**: Premature commitment — locking into one interpretation before accumulating enough instances
- **Slow + Complicated**: Practicing the wrong thing — scarce iterations wasted on non-critical sub-skills
- **Slow + Complex**: Narrative bias — spinning a compelling story from thin data while ignoring base rates
