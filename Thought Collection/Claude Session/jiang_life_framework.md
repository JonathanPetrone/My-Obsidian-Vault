# Jiang's Life Framework

A working framework distilled from Professor Jiang's *Game Theory* and *Great Books* lectures. The geopolitics and book criticism are scaffolding; the load-bearing material is a coherent theory of how to live. This document extracts the load-bearing pieces.

Each principle has the same shape:
- **Model** — the idea, in a sentence.
- **Why** — the reasoning Jiang gives for it.
- **When it applies** — the decision point where you actually reach for it.

Before the principles, there is a **Foundation** — Jiang's theory of consciousness. The practical advice depends on it; without the foundation, the principles read as aphorisms. With it, they read as logical consequences.

---

## Foundation: The Metaphysical Architecture

Every principle that follows rests on a single claim Jiang makes repeatedly: **the universe is consciousness**. Not "contains" or "produces." *Is.* The model is a synthesis of Hermetic philosophy, Kant, Plato, and Eastern thought — not original to him, but stitched together into a working system.

### F1. The monad and vibration
At the center of the universe is **the monad** — the source, the One, God. The monad emanates vibrations. "Vibrations carry information. Information becomes consciousness." (*Great Books 1 — Secrets of the Universe*) Reality is a frequency phenomenon, not a substance one. The material world is what those vibrations look like once they slow down enough to take physical form.

### F2. Your consciousness is infinite-dimensional
At one level, your consciousness is just you. At higher levels, it connects to the people you love. At the highest level, it is identical with the universe. "Our consciousness is infinite. But we don't know that because in order to navigate our reality, we hallucinate time and space." (*Great Books 6 — The Intimacy of Love*) Time and space are not features of reality; they are a UI layer the ego generates so you can operate as a separate self.

### F3. Two hemispheres, two worlds
Borrowing from Kant: the right hemisphere connects to the **noumena** — things-in-themselves, the spiritual, what cannot be directly known. The left hemisphere translates that signal into the **phenomena** — what your senses report. (*Great Books 1*) Most people live entirely in the left-brain output and mistake it for reality. Intuition is the right-brain channel, which is why Jiang trusts it over reason: reason is downstream.

### F4. Three planes of self: mind, spirit, soul
Within you, consciousness has three planes — **mind** (thought), **spirit** (emotion), **soul** (the deep core, the part that knew your purpose before you arrived). When the three align, you are "invincible" (Odysseus restringing the bow). When they conflict, the experience is cognitive dissonance, trauma, depression. The lifelong work is alignment. (*Great Books 6*)

### F5. Imagination is the animating force; attention is the fuel
Reality is constructed by the imagination, and your **attention is the energy that powers the construction**. "Attention is energy." (*Great Books 1*) Whoever directs your attention is therefore building reality on your behalf. This is why Jiang treats social media, AI, and modern schooling as adversarial — not on moral grounds, but on metaphysical ones. They are siphoning the substance you need to imagine your own life.

### F6. Love is gravity back to the source
Love is the force that pulls separate consciousnesses back into unity with the monad. Two people who truly love each other "move together back to the monad, and within the monad their love is imprinted." (*Great Books 6*) This is why intimacy — not obsession — is the real test of love. Intimacy is two consciousnesses actually overlapping; obsession is one consciousness alone, fixated on a target.

### F7. Freedom is waking up from the cave
Plato's cave runs as the central metaphor across the Great Books series. The prisoners watch shadows on a wall and mistake them for reality. **Freedom** is the trained capacity to turn around, walk out, tolerate the initial blindness, and learn to see the real world for what it is. "Freedom is to escape this system and to be able to think for yourself, to see for yourself, to be yourself." (*Great Books 1*) Every spiritual practice in his framework — meditation, reading, suffering through depression, falling in love — is the same operation: waking up.

### A note on how to hold this

The structural claims (mind/spirit/soul, imagination as animating force, attention as energy, the cave) are usable as a working model regardless of what you believe about ultimate reality. They are good frames for navigating a life.

The literal claims (the universe has plans and intervenes, the gods literally engineered Priam's walk into the Greek camp in the Iliad, vibrations literally encode the information of consciousness) are harder to put weight on, and there is no way to argue them. The honest move is to treat the architecture as a useful working model — something to think *with* — rather than as a metaphysical commitment. That preserves the practical power of the principles without forcing you to defend claims you cannot verify.

The principles below assume the structural claims and stay agnostic on the literal ones.

## I. Self & Purpose

### 1. You came here with a purpose; trust your intuition, not your reason
**Model.** Your soul chose a purpose before you arrived. You won't remember it explicitly, but you can *feel* it. Your job is to align with that feeling.

**Why.** "You came here for a purpose. You may not remember what your purpose is, but you can feel what your purpose is. And so what you should always do is trust your intuition because that's what's going to lead you to your purpose." (*Game Theory 29 — Final Examination*) The metaphysical reason this works: intuition is the right-brain channel to the noumena (F3); reason is the left-brain's translation layer, downstream of the actual signal. Trusting reason over intuition is trusting the copy over the original.

**When it applies.** Decisions about direction (career pivots, what to study, where to live, who to marry). Use intuition as the primary signal; use reason to stress-test, not to choose.

### 2. Rock bottom is not a detour — it is the curriculum
**Model.** Periods of depression and disorientation are *required* stages of development, not failures of one. Avoid them and you skip the lesson.

**Why.** Jiang on his own late-20s collapse: "It's not something to be avoided. It's something that you'll have to suffer through. And when you hit rock bottom, you instinctively or intuitively understand that I need to move on in my life… Failure is not something to be ignored or avoided or be to be feared. It's something to be embraced." (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** When you're suffering a real failure or an aimless season. Don't paper it over with productivity. Use it as a forcing function for self-reflection — question the assumptions about the world that brought you here.

### 3. The mind, the spirit, and the soul must align — that alignment is what makes you invincible
**Model.** This is the practical face of F4. Consciousness has three planes — mind (thought), spirit (emotion), soul (the deep core). When all three tell the same story about who you are and what you're doing, you are unstoppable. When they tell different stories, you experience cognitive dissonance, trauma, and depression.

**Why.** Odysseus only restrings the bow — the metaphor for restoring identity — once his soul (he is a husband and father), spirit (he loves Penelope and is loved back), and mind (he has a plan to kill the suitors) all agree. "The alignment of his mind, his soul and his spirit which makes him invincible." (*Great Books 6 — The Intimacy of Love*)

**When it applies.** Diagnostic, not prescriptive. When something feels off, ask which plane is out of sync: does your *mind* believe one story while your *gut* believes another? Re-alignment is the work. Suffering is almost always a misalignment signal, not a failure signal.

### 4. Believe in yourself — that is the actual highest mission
**Model.** Self-belief is not a confidence trick or a productivity hack. It is the foundational fact you build a life on. Without it, no university, no salary, no résumé saves you.

**Why.** When asked what he tells his own children every day, Jiang gives three things: "I will always love you… I believe in you… just believe in yourself." Then: "It's not really about what university you should go to. It's not really about how much money you have. It's really about how you perceive yourself, your place in the world." (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** When you're optimizing the wrong variable — credentials, income, prestige — at the expense of self-perception. Re-anchor: what am I actually building here?

## II. Mind & Reality

### 5. Imagination is the animating force of the universe — whoever captures yours owns your reality
**Model.** The direct practical statement of F5. Reality is not a fixed external thing you observe. It is constructed by the imagination, and your attention is the energy that powers the construction. Whoever directs your attention is therefore building your reality.

**Why.** "Attention is energy… reality is what we imagine it to be. The imagination is the animating force of the universe. If they can focus our attention for us, they can create their own reality." (*Great Books 1*) This is why movies, social media, school, and AI exist — not to inform you, but to capture your imaginative energy and spend it on a reality someone else designed. The threat is not surveillance. The threat is being run as a battery for someone else's universe.

**When it applies.** Audit where your attention actually goes. The feed is not neutral. Reclaiming attention is reclaiming the capacity to imagine your own life.

### 6. Freedom is the ability to think, see, and be yourself
**Model.** The lived form of F7. Freedom is not the absence of constraints. It is the trained ability to step outside the cave, tolerate the initial blindness, and learn to see the real world for what it is.

**Why.** Riffing on Plato: "Freedom is to escape this system and to be able to think for yourself, to see for yourself, to be yourself." At first the light is painful and you hate it; eventually you teach your eyes to see and recognize that what you took for reality "is just a corpse… a dead zombie world." (*Great Books 1*) Freedom is therefore an *acquired capacity*, not a default state — most people will live their entire lives inside the cave because the cave is comfortable and the light hurts.

**When it applies.** Any moment of consensus-without-evidence — when you find yourself believing something because everyone around you believes it. That is the cave. The discomfort of disagreeing is the light hitting your eyes.

### 7. Read books — the medium matters more than the subject
**Model.** What you study is largely irrelevant. *That* you read books at all is the entire point, because reading is the only mass-available technology that produces sustained inner dialogue.

**Why.** "It doesn't matter what you study, but what it does matter is to read books. By reading books, you can start to engage in an inner dialogue with yourself. And that's going to make you much more imaginative, much more resilient in the future." (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** When you're optimizing your input diet. Choose books over feeds, not because books are higher-status, but because the format forces the inner conversation that everything else suppresses.

## III. Action & Power

### 8. The point is to win the game — not to survive it
**Model.** Survival and winning are different objectives that recommend different tactics. Most people quietly default to survival and call it strategy. Real players play to win.

**Why.** "In a game, your objective is to win the game. Now by winning the game it may cause the game to collapse, but you don't care. The point is to win the game and you'll use whatever tools you have in order to win the game… The point is not to survive the game. The point is to win the game. And sometimes winning the game means destroying yourself." (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** Whenever you catch yourself optimizing for "not losing." Ask: am I playing to win this, or playing to not get kicked out? If it's the latter, you've already lost on a longer timescale.

### 9. Open, Energetic, Cohesive — the triad that lets you rise from nothing
**Model.** Three traits predict whether a person or a group can ascend from a weak position: *open* (willing to learn from mistakes and admit when wrong), *energetic* (works seriously, sacrifices for outcomes), *cohesive* (acts as part of a group with shared sacrifice). The opposite — *insular, corrupt, divided* — is the signature of decline.

**Why.** Jiang's whole theory of how borderlands conquer empires runs on this. "If you want to go from being poor to becoming rich, you have to be open, energetic, and cohesive." The empire's factions become insular, corrupt, and divided; the borderlands win on the triad. (*Game Theory 5 — The World Game*)

**When it applies.** As a diagnostic on yourself, your team, or any organization you're committing to. The moment any of the three flips, decline has started. Resources don't predict outcomes — these three traits do.

### 10. You don't need resources — the poorest position is often the most creative
**Model.** Scarcity is not a disqualifier. The team or person starting with the least is often the most creative precisely because they have no choice.

**Why.** "I keep telling you guys, you don't need resources. You can cheat, you can steal, you can beg… whichever team starts off with the least resources are often the most creative." (*Game Theory 5*)

**When it applies.** When you're waiting on funding, credentials, permission, or the "right" conditions before starting. The waiting is the disqualification. Constraint is the input to creativity, not an obstacle to it.

### 11. Embrace change — make yourself useful or be discarded
**Model.** The world is not returning to its prior state. The question is no longer "how do I preserve my position" but "what makes me useful in the world that's emerging."

**Why.** "You must embrace change… A natural draft means that if you're useful, they'll keep you alive. If you're not useful, they'll send you to Iraq where you'll die. So make yourself useful. Read books, learn skills, work hard. That's my advice. I'm sorry to be so blunt, but that's the world we live in." (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** When you notice yourself defending an old model — of work, of the economy, of how careers progress. Stop defending. Re-ask what skill or judgment is actually scarce in the world that's coming.

### 12. You are always changing because the game is always changing
**Model.** Identity is not fixed. You behave the way you do because of the game you're currently embedded in. Move to a new game and you become a new person.

**Why.** "We are always changing because the game is always changing. You must adapt to new games that have different players and have new requirements… we are who we are is often determined by the nature of the game." (*Game Theory 2 — Why Schools Suck*)

**When it applies.** When you find yourself behaving in ways you don't endorse. Don't try to change yourself first — change the game. Change the room, the colleagues, the incentives. The behavior will follow.

## IV. Relationships & Love

### 13. Love is intimacy — the shared private language, not the obsession
**Model.** The practical face of F6. You don't know you love someone because you think about them all the time. You know because you understand them intimately — because there are code words, shared secrets, and a private language only the two of you can read.

**Why.** The whole climax of the Odyssey is built on this: Penelope recognizes Odysseus through a single coded word ("brooch") about a memory only they share. The opposite case — Helen and Menelaus — shows two people who tell stories at each other but never actually hear each other. "You love this person if you understand this person intimately." (*Great Books 6*) In the metaphysical frame: intimacy is two consciousnesses actually overlapping at a higher dimension. Obsession is one consciousness alone, fixated on a target.

**When it applies.** Diagnostic for any close relationship. Is there a private language? Are you actually hearing each other or shouting past each other? Possession and obsession can coexist with total non-recognition.

### 14. Connection to the source is the highest happiness — not money, fame, sex, or power
**Model.** The peak experiences in human life are not achievements. They are connection — hugging someone you love, caring for a friend. The reason these moments register as "highest" is that they are literally moments of consciousness rejoining itself (F1, F2). Material achievements are a distraction from the actual thing.

**Why.** "Connection to the monad is the greatest happiness in the world. So moments of greatest happiness in our world are when we hug someone we love, when we care for a friend… You don't need money to be happy. You don't need anything to be happy. You have yourself, your imagination." (*Great Books 1*, *Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** When you're trading present connection for future achievement. The achievement was never the goal — connection was the goal all along, and the achievement was a story you told yourself about *how* to get to connection.

### 15. To love anyone else, first love yourself
**Model.** Self-love is the prerequisite, not the reward. You cannot give from an empty source; you cannot truly love God, a partner, or a child until you have done the cleansing work on yourself.

**Why.** Dante in purgatory: "If you truly love God, you must first love yourself. That means cleansing yourself of all sins so that you are worthy to be with God." (*Great Books 10 — Dante's Hierarchy of Hell*)

**When it applies.** When you find yourself trying to fix a relationship by giving more, working harder, sacrificing further. The leak is upstream. Address the self-relationship first; the others will follow.

## V. Money, Class & Mobility

### 16. The marshmallow test measures trust, not self-control
**Model.** Most behavior labeled as poor self-discipline is actually rational behavior in a low-trust environment. Poor kids eat the marshmallow not because they lack willpower, but because they have correctly learned that authority figures don't keep promises.

**Why.** "Marshmallow test is not a test of self-control. It's a test of your trust in others… So it's not really about self-control. It's about your belief and trust in authority figures… Poor kids are rational and they're responding to the circumstances that they live in." (*Game Theory 3 — Rich Dad Poor Dad*)

**When it applies.** When you're judging someone (or yourself) for impulsivity, short-termism, or "bad" decisions. Ask first whether the environment ever rewarded patience. If not, the behavior is the right answer to the actual question being asked.

### 17. Rich kids learn to negotiate. Poor kids learn to obey. Mobility requires switching modes
**Model.** Class isn't only about money. It's about which mode you were trained in: obedience to authority, or negotiation with authority. The poor person who never learns to negotiate stays poor.

**Why.** "As a poor person, if you want to survive, you have to obey authority. But if you're a rich person, the way that you get along with others, the way that you maximize your outcome is by negotiating with others." Rich parents teach debate; poor parents teach obedience — each is locally optimal in its own world. (*Game Theory 3*)

**When it applies.** Career, salary, partnerships, vendor relationships. If your default response to authority is compliance, you've been trained for a different game than the one you're trying to win. Practice debating *with respect* — that's the move.

### 18. To rise, you must abandon your community
**Model.** Your community will tolerate small ascents. Real upward mobility almost always requires leaving — geographically, socially, or both — because staying inside the community guarantees regression to its mean.

**Why.** "One way to succeed is to abandon your community. But that's high risk… you have to be extremely individualistic to take such a risk. So that's why most people don't do it… the safest option for you is to stay within your community." (*Game Theory 3*)

**When it applies.** When you're trying to grow without changing your environment. The environment is the ceiling. Expect ostracism. Plan for it. "Because we do this, we have no friends in China. We have family, but they all think we're crazy."

### 19. Debt is the modern slavery — sufficiency is the modern freedom
**Model.** The structural marker of freedom across history is not income but *absence of debt* combined with *ability to feed yourself*. Debt is the lever every system uses to convert nominally free people into compliant ones.

**Why.** "[In Muslim law] if you are a Muslim, you can never be a slave. Look at every single revolution — over time, because of inequality in society… If you want to become king, what the first uniting king always does is cancel all debts. Because if you cancel all debts, who loses power? It's the rich who lose power." (*Game Theory 3*)

**When it applies.** When evaluating financial decisions, weight debt-elimination and self-sufficiency higher than yield or growth. Whoever holds your debt holds your option set.

## VI. Learning & Growth

### 20. You must experience hell to understand heaven; you must misunderstand to understand
**Model.** Goodness, truth, and virtue are not accessible directly. They are only legible by contrast — you have to confront evil, error, and sin to actually know what their opposites mean.

**Why.** Dante doesn't get to paradise without first walking through hell with Virgil. "You must first confront evil. You must first understand sin for you to truly understand goodness… If you want to understand, you first have to misunderstand. If you want to be virtuous, you must first sin." (*Great Books 9 — Dante*, *Great Books 10*)

**When it applies.** When you're trying to teach (or learn) something abstract — virtue, taste, craft, ethics. Don't try to instill the positive directly. Walk through the failure modes first; the positive becomes obvious by contrast.

### 21. All true innovation is cultic — heroes believe in a secret mission
**Model.** Every founder, scientist, and prophet of consequence operates with the inner conviction that they are on a private mission, often a divine one, and they build a small group of believers around them. This is not a deviation from rationality — it is the engine.

**Why.** "All true innovation is a cult in nature. You have to understand the cult if you want to be innovative… Heroic figures in history all believe that they are on a mission, a secret mission from God and they must create a movement around them in order to advance humanity." (*Game Theory 29*, on Joseph Campbell)

**When it applies.** When you're working on something genuinely original and feel embarrassed about how much conviction it requires. That conviction is the actual fuel. Don't try to be more reasonable about it. Try to be more disciplined about it.

### 22. Free will is sacred — even when other people use it badly
**Model.** The fact that some people use free will to dominate and exploit is not a bug; it is the cost of a universe in which anyone can be truly creative. You cannot have one without the other.

**Why.** "God has given us free will. Free will means you can do good, you can do evil… The thing to remember is that God wants it this way because only by giving us complete free will can we be truly imaginative. I couldn't be doing what I'm doing unless there are evil people like Mark Zuckerberg doing what he's doing." (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** When you're tempted to argue for systems that constrain everyone else's choices in order to prevent bad outcomes. The constraint also eliminates the upside. Pick the open system and tolerate the cost.

## VII. Ethics & Daily Action

### 23. Honor, Justice, Truth — the three reasons worth acting on
**Model.** When deciding whether to act, three reasons are enough: honor (legacy, who you are to those who come after you), justice (right action regardless of outcome), and truth (alignment with what is real).

**Why.** Telemachus's reason for setting out is to build a legacy for his future son: "This is how you should live your life. You should live a life of honor, of justice, of truth." (*Great Books 5 — The Odyssey*)

**When it applies.** When the calculation of pros and cons is paralyzing. Skip the calculation and check which of the three reasons applies. If one does, act. If none does, don't.

### 24. Don't label — focus on what you can do individually
**Model.** Don't waste cycles deciding whether large abstractions (America, the system, capitalism, the elite) are good or bad. Spend the cycles on the small radius of action where you can actually do something good.

**Why.** "Our role, our purpose in this world is to navigate these patterns to seek our own individual meaning… you have a responsibility to be a good teacher, to be a good parent, to be a good neighbor. Focus on what you can do individually and don't give labels to these things. Is America good or bad?" (*Game Theory 29*)

**When it applies.** Any time you're deep in a political or systems-level argument and notice you have done nothing concrete this week. The argument is the avoidance. Return to the radius of one — the people you actually touch.

### 25. The game is always between "have a lot" and "have some" — never between rich and poor
**Model.** Real conflict in history is never between the very top and the very bottom. It is between the very top and the *aspirational middle* — the people with some power who want more. Recognize which slot you're in.

**Why.** "All revolutions are always between the have-a-lot versus have-some. It's never between rich and poor. It's always between have-a-lot and have-some… If you look at every revolution in human history, that's always been the case." (*Game Theory 3*)

**When it applies.** When you're analyzing power dynamics — at work, in markets, in politics. The story of "the masses rise up" is almost always wrong. Look for the ambitious middle. They are the agent of change.

## Closing Note

These twenty-five principles are not Jiang's system. He doesn't have a system in the published sense — he has a sensibility, scattered across forty-one lectures about wars, books, and empires, anchored on the consciousness theory in the Foundation. The framework above is one attempt to compress that sensibility into something you can actually use on a Tuesday morning.

The structure is two-layered on purpose:
- The **Foundation** is the metaphysical claim — usable as a working model even if you stay agnostic on its literal truth.
- The **Principles** are the practical consequences — they hold up regardless of whether you ever fully accept the metaphysics, because most of them have independent evidence in psychology, history, or your own life.

If the framework starts feeling rigid, the load-bearing meta-principle is the one Jiang repeats most often: **you came here for a purpose, you can feel what it is, trust your intuition, and don't let anyone — including this document — capture your imagination on your behalf.**
