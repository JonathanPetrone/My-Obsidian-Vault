#### Examples:
- Long-term investing
- Diplomatic relationship building
- Personal knowledge management system
### Slow Feedback:
- **Irreversible/expensive iterations**: Mistakes consume significant resources (time/money/materials/opportunity)
- **Long validation cycles**: Months to decades before outcomes clear
- **Attempt scarcity**: Dozens or fewer full attempts in a lifetime
- **Delayed error detection**: Mistakes may not surface until far downstream
- **Compressed practice**: Career might span only 10-30 complete iterations
- **Ambiguous causation**: Hard to isolate what caused success/failure due to temporal distance
- **Survivor bias risk**: Only see results from those who lasted long enough
### Complex:
- **Emergent properties**: System behavior not predictable from components alone
- **Non-stationary**: Patterns evolve, rules rewrite themselves over time
- **Reflexive**: Observer/participant actions change the system
- **Context-dependent**: What works in situation A fails in situation B for non-obvious reasons
- **Irreducible uncertainty**: Genuine novelty emerges, not just unknown-but-knowable facts
- **Multiple stable states**: Same initial conditions can lead to divergent outcomes
- **Network effects**: Relationships between elements matter more than elements themselves
- **Adaptive adversaries**: System responds to your strategies (markets, social systems)

### Navigating Slow + Complex Domains

Slow feedback in a complex environment is the hardest combination. You can't iterate cheaply and you can't learn stable rules — cause-effect is emergent, the system co-evolves with your actions, and by the time you find out whether a decision was right, the conditions that shaped it may have already changed. A career might span only 10-30 complete iterations. The goal isn't to accumulate instances through volume (you can't) but to extract maximum learning from scarce experience while maintaining enough optionality that no single failure is catastrophic.

The structure for navigating this draws heavily from [[The Process of Superforecasting|Tetlock's superforecasting process]], which was built for exactly this problem — making good judgments in complex environments where feedback is delayed and ambiguous. Where Fast + Complex uses SBTM's exploration cycle, Slow + Complex uses a forecasting cycle: Map → Position → Monitor → Update → Reposition.

**Map** — [[Decomposing Big Questions - Fermi's Method|Decompose]] the big slow question into smaller components where signal arrives faster. In Slow + Complex, the most useful form is decomposition for diagnosis — breaking vague outcomes into observable leading indicators. "Will this investment thesis play out?" becomes "are the early adoption signals I'd expect present? Are the market conditions shifting in the direction the thesis requires?" You're creating faster sub-loops within the slow outer loop. Start by [[Anchor Initial Assessment to Base Rates|anchoring to base rates]] — what happens in situations like this one in general? — then layer in the case-specific details. This fights the natural temptation to spin a compelling narrative from thin data while ignoring how common or rare the outcome actually is.

**Position** — Since iterations are scarce and expensive, you can't afford to bet everything on one interpretation. Place multiple small positions where downside is limited and upside is disproportionate — Taleb's asymmetric risk logic. Favor reversible experiments over irreversible commitments. The portfolio approach hedges against the irreducible uncertainty that defines complex domains: you don't know which position will pay off, but you know that concentrating on a single bet in a non-stationary system is how you get wiped out.

**Monitor** — Watch the leading indicators you identified during mapping. Since feedback is delayed and ambiguous, you're looking for weak signals rather than clear confirmation. [[Think in Probabilities and Numbers|Thinking in probabilities]] matters here — it forces you to commit to specific beliefs and track whether your model is actually shifting over time, rather than hiding behind "it's too early to tell." The difference between 60% and 70% confidence is invisible in casual thinking but meaningful over many slow decisions.

**Update** — When new information arrives, [[Bayesian Mindset and Bayes Theorem|update your model]] rather than abandoning or defending it. The Bayesian discipline is the epistemic posture for Slow + Complex, the same way Fox thinking is the posture for Fast + Complex. Both resist premature commitment, but through different mechanisms: Fox thinking holds multiple lenses simultaneously in real-time, Bayesian thinking holds a probability and adjusts it incrementally as signal trickles in over months or years. Ask: what was my prior? How much should this new evidence shift me? What's my updated probability?

**Reposition** — As your model updates, adjust your portfolio of positions. Close bets that the evidence is weighing against. Double down where signal is accumulating. Open new positions in directions you didn't anticipate at the start. Each repositioning is itself an expensive move in a slow domain, so the threshold for action should be higher than in Fast + Complex — but not so high that you're frozen by the cost of being wrong.

Because direct experience is scarce in this quadrant, vicarious learning carries more weight than anywhere else. Intensively study others' experiences — case studies, mentorship, historical analysis. You're borrowing their instances to supplement a pattern library your own experience can't fill. Pre-mortem analysis is also disproportionately valuable here: before committing to an expensive irreversible move, imagine it failed and work backwards to find why. It surfaces blind spots you can't afford to discover through trial and error when iterations are this rare.

---

**TLDR — Slow + Complex Navigation Loop (Superforecasting for learning):**
Decompose the big question into observable leading indicators. Anchor to base rates before layering in case specifics. Position across multiple small bets with limited downside. Monitor leading indicators and track beliefs as probabilities. Update via Bayesian adjustment when new signal arrives — don't abandon or defend, adjust. Reposition your portfolio as evidence accumulates. Supplement scarce direct experience with intensive vicarious learning. Pre-mortem before expensive commitments.
