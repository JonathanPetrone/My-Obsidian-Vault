#### Examples:
- Teaching/lecturing
- Auction bidding strategy
- Wildfire firefighting tactics
### Fast Feedback
- **Iteration frequency**: Minutes to days between attempts
- **Reversibility**: Low-cost failures, easy reset to try again
- **Attempt volume**: Hundreds/thousands of reps possible per year
- **Error visibility**: Mistakes immediately obvious (wrong move, missed note, failed test)
- **Practice density**: Can achieve 10,000 hours in 5 years of deliberate practice
- **Learning curve shape**: Rapid initial improvement visible within weeks
- **Skill verification**: Can confirm mastery through repeated demonstration in short timeframe
### Complex:
- **Emergent properties**: System behavior not predictable from components alone
- **Non-stationary**: Patterns evolve, rules rewrite themselves over time
- **Reflexive**: Observer/participant actions change the system
- **Context-dependent**: What works in situation A fails in situation B for non-obvious reasons
- **Irreducible uncertainty**: Genuine novelty emerges, not just unknown-but-knowable facts
- **Multiple stable states**: Same initial conditions can lead to divergent outcomes
- **Network effects**: Relationships between elements matter more than elements themselves
- **Adaptive adversaries**: System responds to your strategies (markets, social systems)

### Navigating Fast + Complex Domains

Fast feedback in a complex environment means you can generate signal cheaply — but the signal is ambiguous. Cause-effect isn't stable, the system reacts to your actions, and what worked yesterday may not work tomorrow. The goal isn't to learn rules but to build pattern recognition fast enough to stay ahead of the environment's evolution.

The structure for navigating this maps closely to James Bach's Session-Based Test Management (SBTM), which was designed for the same reason — scripted approaches fail when you can't predict what you'll find. Bach's [[Buccaneer-Scholar Self-Education Method]] is essentially his learning philosophy; SBTM is that philosophy given operational structure. Applied to learning in any fast + complex domain, the cycle looks like this:

**Charter** — Before each session, declare what you're exploring and why. This is the scoping move. Tight enough to focus your probes, loose enough to follow surprises when the system shows you something unexpected. Early charters are broad ("how does this system respond to X?"). Later charters sharpen as patterns emerge ("is the pattern I'm seeing in Y context-dependent or stable?").

**Session** — A time-boxed burst of direct engagement with authentic problems. Authentic means the consequences are real, the context is messy and unsimplified, and the feedback comes from the system itself rather than an evaluator's interpretation. You enter with [[The General System Thinkers Approach|naive curiosity]] — stay general, take grand leaps, expect to be wrong fast. Each action within the session is a probe: a small reversible move designed to learn how the system responds, not to validate a prediction. In early sessions, probe widely. Don't try to interpret each result inline — accumulate raw observations.

**Debrief** — Step back and make sense of what happened. This is where [[Perspectives - Hedgehogs vs. Foxes|Fox thinking]] kicks in: before committing to an interpretation, name the lens you're using and generate 2-3 alternative explanations for the same observation. The goal is falsification of your own emerging conclusions before they harden into beliefs. Then run the reflection protocol: What did I expect? What actually happened? What does the gap tell me about the system? What pattern does this connect to across previous sessions? Weick's research on high-reliability organizations shows that understanding in complex environments is always retrospective — you can't plan forward, but you can make sense of what happened and let that reshape your orientation.

**Adjust** — The debrief reshapes the next charter. Each cycle sharpens what you're looking at. The first session is basically exploring in the dark. By the fifth, you're targeting specific aspects of the system because your pattern library is forming.

Over enough cycles, what accumulates is largely [[Thought Collection/Books (Single source and note)/The Tacit Dimension - M.Polanyi/Tacit Knowledge|tacit]] — pre-verbal pattern recognition from authentic encounters. Klein's research on firefighters and military commanders (Recognition-Primed Decision Making) shows where this leads: experts stop analytically comparing options. They recognize a pattern, mentally simulate a response, and if the simulation doesn't break, they act. The speed of expertise isn't thinking faster — it's having enough accumulated instances that recognition replaces analysis. Boyd's OODA loop captures the same idea from a different angle — the person who reorients faster wins, not the person who acts faster.

Patterns that survive stress-testing across enough sessions can eventually compress into simple heuristics. Gigerenzer's work on ecological rationality shows that in complex environments, simple decision rules often outperform elaborate analytical models because they don't overfit to noise. These heuristics are the output of the cycle, not an input to it — they're earned through accumulated authentic experience, not borrowed from someone else's framework.

---

**TLDR — Fast + Complex Navigation Loop (SBTM for learning):**
Work on authentic problems (real stakes, messy context, system feedback). Charter what you're exploring. Probe in time-boxed bursts — small reversible actions to learn, not to validate. Debrief by falsifying your first interpretations through multiple lenses, then reflect: expected → actual → gap meaning → cross-instance patterns. Adjust the next charter based on what emerged. Repeat until tacit recognition replaces conscious analysis. Compress surviving patterns into simple heuristics.
