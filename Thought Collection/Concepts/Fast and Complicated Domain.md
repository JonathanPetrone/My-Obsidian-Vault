### Fast Feedback
- **Iteration frequency**: Minutes to days between attempts
- **Reversibility**: Low-cost failures, easy reset to try again
- **Attempt volume**: Hundreds/thousands of reps possible per year
- **Error visibility**: Mistakes immediately obvious (wrong move, missed note, failed test)
- **Practice density**: Can achieve 10,000 hours in 5 years of deliberate practice
- **Learning curve shape**: Rapid initial improvement visible within weeks
- **Skill verification**: Can confirm mastery through repeated demonstration in short timeframe
### Complicated:
- **Deterministic/probabilistic but stable**: Same inputs → same outputs (or predictable distributions)
- **Decomposable**: Can break into analyzable sub-components
- **Teachable through rules**: Expertise can be systematically transmitted via procedures
- **Pattern libraries exist**: Catalogues of solutions to known problem types
- **Expertise compounds predictably**: More experience → monotonically better performance
- **Best practices converge**: Experts agree on "right" approaches
- **Mistakes have systematic causes**: Failures are analyzable, preventable through better technique
- **Bounded problem space**: Known unknowns, not unknown unknowns

### Material that is helpful in this domain:
- [[Principles of Ultralearning]] The author assumes you can define what mastery looks like upfront (languages, programming, portrait drawing). That's complicated-domain thinking - knowable problem space with convergent best practices.

### Navigating Fast + Complicated Domains

Fast feedback in a complicated environment is the most efficient quadrant for building expertise. Cause-effect is stable and knowable, mistakes are immediately visible, and you can accumulate thousands of reps. The rules exist, experts agree on them, and practice compounds predictably. The risk here isn't getting the wrong strategy — it's wasting time on inefficient practice when the conditions for rapid improvement are ideal.

The structure for navigating this is [[Principles of Ultralearning|Ultralearning's]] skill-building cycle, which assumes exactly this environment: a knowable problem space where you can define mastery upfront and practice your way there.

**Map** — [[Ultralearners 1st principle - metalearning|Metalearning]] comes first. Research WHY you're learning this (instrumental or intrinsic), WHAT needs to be learned (concepts to understand, facts to memorize, procedures to practice), and HOW others have learned it (benchmark against existing curricula or expert paths). In fast + complicated domains this step can be relatively quick because the territory is well-charted — pattern libraries exist and best practices converge.

**Practice directly** — [[Ultralearners 3rd principle - directness|Directness]] means tying your practice to the actual context you want to perform in. Don't theorize about regex patterns — write regex patterns. Don't study Rubik's algorithms abstractly — solve cubes. The transfer problem is real: knowledge learned in one context doesn't automatically apply in another, especially when your knowledge base is still thin. Direct practice in the target environment builds the right connections from the start.

**Drill** — [[Ultralearners 4th principle - drills|Isolate the rate-limiting sub-skills]] and attack them separately. The Direct-then-Drill cycle: practice the whole skill directly, identify which component is bottlenecking your performance, isolate and drill that component, then integrate back into direct practice. Early in learning, keep these cycles short and frequent. As you approach mastery, longer drill detours become more viable because you have enough whole-skill context to know what matters.

**Retrieve and verify** — [[Ultralearners 5th principle - retrieval|Retrieval practice]] is more effective than passive review because it exposes the gap between what you think you know and what you actually know. The struggle of trying to recall is itself the learning mechanism — if it feels easy, you're probably not learning. Test yourself before you feel ready. Design self-generated challenges from passive material. The [[Ultralearners 6th principle - feedback|feedback]] you seek should be as corrective as possible — not just "right/wrong" (outcome feedback) but "what's wrong and how to fix it" (corrective feedback). In fast + complicated domains this kind of feedback is readily available through knowledgeable tutors, established benchmarks, and immediate results.

**Compress and automate** — As patterns become reliable through practice, they chunk into automatic responses. This is where Barbara Oakley's focused/diffuse alternation matters — focused mode for drilling and deliberate practice, diffuse mode for letting chunks consolidate and connect. The end state in this quadrant is genuine automaticity: the skill becomes effortless not because it's easy but because the patterns are so deeply practiced they no longer require conscious processing.

---

**TLDR — Fast + Complicated Navigation Loop (Ultralearning for skill-building):**
Map the domain first: WHY, WHAT, HOW. Practice directly in the target context — don't theorize when you can do. Isolate rate-limiting sub-skills and drill them, then integrate back into whole practice. Retrieve aggressively to expose gaps between perceived and actual knowledge. Seek corrective feedback, not just outcome feedback. As patterns solidify, let them chunk into automaticity through focused/diffuse alternation.
